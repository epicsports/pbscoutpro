## 0. The one-line bet

**We are not launching an app. We are founding a category — "paintball intelligence" — and
Reads is how you practice it. And we let the market, not our own claims, decide whether
it's real.** Founding the category beats launching a product because the category has no
name yet; whoever defines it owns the language the whole sport uses. Education comes first:
teach the thinking → become the name → the product is the obvious delivery mechanism.

---

## 1. Why the category is open

In mainstream sport, analytics is assumed — xG in football, advanced stats in basketball.
In competitive paintball it barely exists as a named practice; the knowledge lives in
coaches' heads and notes on a knee. The discipline is real and practiced informally by the
best teams — nobody has *named it, structured it, and made it teachable*. We do that. The
brand already carries the worldview ("Reads — Paintball Intelligence", "Make the read");
this just unfolds it into something legible and repeatable.

---

## 2. What Paintball Intelligence *is* — the beliefs

Everything we publish rests on five ideas. Sell these, not the app.

1. **The read is the unit of paintball.** Every point is a sequence of reads — the break,
   the lane, the opponent's tendency, the endgame. Better reads win points. The team that
   sees first, wins first.
2. **Reads can be trained, not only felt.** Patterns repeat, teams have tendencies, fields
   have geometry. Study it and you prepare the read in advance instead of improvising it
   under fire.
3. **The bottleneck is time, not data.** Every serious team already gathers observations.
   The edge isn't whether the data exists — it's whether you can turn it into a clear read
   in the two minutes you actually have between matches.
4. **The tool serves the coach; it does not replace him.** The read stays human. The app
   removes the clerical load — gathering, consolidating, formatting — so the coach spends
   scarce minutes on *judgment*, and his read reaches all five players at once, clearly,
   instead of dying in a rushed briefing. It **scales** the coach's influence; it doesn't
   dilute it.
5. **It puts a pro-level workflow in reach of any serious team.** The top programs already
   do this informally with deep staffs. The tool puts the *workflow* — not a secret — within
   reach of any team that takes the game seriously.

---

## 3. The value, in one line — *speed to clarity*

> **The app doesn't give you knowledge. It gives you back your own scouting —
> consolidated and legible — in the window where you can actually use it, so the whole
> team can read it together.**

A coach with a notebook and an hour would have the same picture. He doesn't have an hour;
he has two minutes after the match. The app closes the gap between **observation and
decision**. That gap-closing — *speed to clarity* — is the real edge, and it's the one an
insider can't dispute, because it claims to create nothing the team didn't already earn. It
is also exactly *why* the read stays the coach's: the app does the clerical part so he can
do the judgment part, and so his read reaches the whole team at once.

This is the heart of the positioning. When in doubt, come back to this sentence.

## 4. The five pillars — "Read the ___"

The brand verb, unfolded into a content series. **The order is deliberate — it is also the
conceptual map of the product** (field → break → opponent → player → whole game). Teaching
the category in this sequence *is* walking someone through the app without selling it.

| Pillar | Teaches (the concept) | Reveals (the surface, quietly) |
|---|---|---|
| **Read the field** | Layout geometry, lanes, bunker roles, danger zones — spatial thinking | The zone / layout system |
| **Read the break** | Breakout theory, primaries, why teams break the way they do | The tactic board |
| **Read the opponent** | Tendencies, how patterns repeat across points | Scouting capture + heatmaps |
| **Read the player** | Effectiveness, positions, hitability — what makes a position dangerous | Player stats |
| **Read the game** | Full-point / full-match teardown through the intelligence lens | The whole loop — the payoff |

**"Read the game" is the flagship — and it carries the whole posture.** It's a real point
or match broken down through the intelligence lens. **Don't sell it with credentials — let
the teardown's quality speak.** Provenance, if mentioned at all, stays generic — *built and
proven inside real top-level competition* — **never a win-claim, never naming the team or
event.** (Naming it would both over-claim and, given the access rules in §6, tip off the
very competitors we're gating out.)

**Format law:** one concept = one image = one "aha." Short, visual, finishable in seconds.
Never teach the whole sport in one post — the gap between posts is where appetite lives.

## 5. Voice & tone

**The split (non-negotiable):** this voice is marketing/education only. Product UI stays
functional and translatable. Two registers, never mixed.

The marketing voice is **a sharp coach who respects you** — not a hype account.

- **Confident, not loud.** We know this is real. We don't shout it.
- **Teacher, not salesman.** Every post leaves you knowing something. The "sell" is that
  you now want more of the thinking.
- **Show, don't tell.** A clean opponent heatmap does more than a paragraph of claims. The
  conversion moment is a coach looking at it and thinking *"I need that for my team."*
- **Let the market judge.** We show the tool in real use and let teams decide if it's
  valuable or a gadget. We don't assert worth — we demonstrate it and invite the verdict.
  Confident enough to be judged.
- **Calm and analytical.** The aesthetic of intelligence is restraint. Let the work be the
  drama.

**Don't:** hype-speak ("GAME CHANGER 🔥"), emoji-as-chrome, trash-talk, fake scarcity,
win-claims we can't (and shouldn't) make, tutorials that give away the coaching craft.

## 6. Who this is for, and where we're allowed to sell

**The buying unit is the team (the workspace), not the individual.** A team adopts; its
players use it. Team count is the primary market metric.

### The landscape
Competitive tournament paintball runs on **Xball** (timed, point-based, inflatable-bunker
speedball). Its global backbone is **PBLI / pbleagues.com** — the registration + results
platform nearly every competitive team passes through. Strategically huge: **the entire
addressable market is enumerable in one database** and shows up at the same handful of
events. **NXL (National Xball League)** is the dominant body: per MLPB, US majors average
**230+ teams/event** and the **NXL World Cup draws 500+ teams from dozens of countries**.
Pyramid D5 → D1 → Semi-Pro → Pro; ~4 majors + World Cup per season. Dozens of regional /
national leagues also run on PBLI across 10+ countries.

**Honest framing:** even as "the biggest sport you've never heard of," competitive paintball
is **niche** — passionate, knowledgeable, but small. For our strategy that's a feature.

### The access rule (defines our reachable market)
> **🔒 No app access for European teams while Jacek competes for Ranger.** Enforced by not
> issuing magic-link invites to European teams — the invite system *is* the gate. This
> protects Ranger's competitive edge from rivals on its own circuit.

Content is global and **cannot** be geo-blocked from *viewing* — that's fine. The
consequence: the **access line is the moat, not the content** (so don't broadcast the
coaching methodology Europe could copy — see §9).

### Sizing estimate (Fermi — orders of magnitude, not a census)
- **TAM — all tournament/Xball teams globally:** ~**2,000–4,000 teams** (~12,000–25,000
  players at ~6–8 active per roster).
- **SAM — serious, analytics-receptive teams** (Pro / Semi-Pro / D1–D3, has a coach,
  practices with intent): ~**300–800 teams**, and **US-heavy** — NXL US is the deepest,
  most analytics-mature slice of this.
- **Reachable SAM (given the Europe lock):** **US-centric**, plus Canada / Latin America.
  Europe is excluded *by our own rule*, not by market size — and since the US alone is the
  largest, most receptive slice, the lock costs us very little reachable market.
- **SOM — realistic reach this year (closed beta):** **single digits to ~15 teams**,
  US-anchored. <2% of SAM — trivially achievable on the *demand* side. The true cap is our
  **operational capacity + Firestore-read headroom on Spark**, not market size (see
  `PRODUCTIZATION_STRATEGY.md` / read-volume audit).

### The strategic takeaway
The market is **small, enumerable, reachable, and physically concentrated** — the entire
serious market fits inside one venue (World Cup) and follows the same handful of accounts.
For a niche like this, **quality > quantity is the correct strategy, not a compromise.** We
don't need a land-grab; we need the serious US teams, one cohort at a time.

## 7. Channels — US-first (ranked)

1. **Instagram — primary organic.** Center of gravity for competitive paintball; our visual
   outputs (heatmaps, boards) are native to the format. This is where "Read the ___" runs.
2. **Facebook groups — reach the decision-makers.** US regional league + "tournament
   paintball" groups, where coaches and captains (who *buy*) live. Less sexy, high intent.
3. **YouTube — category-building credibility.** Long-form "Read the game" teardowns earn
   the *intelligence* reputation — the asset that makes us the authority, not just a feed.
4. **US events.** World Cup (Nov, Kissimmee) and NXL US regionals concentrate the whole
   serious market. Note: physical presence is harder from Poland (travel) — which raises the
   value of the next item.
5. **FIT (Paintball Fit, Texas) as the US foothold.** Not just our first customer — for a
   Europe-based founder, an engaged US customer is the on-the-ground beachhead, reference,
   and word-of-mouth seed *in the right market*. Lean on it deliberately.
6. **PBLI as a target list (not an ad channel).** It maps our audience — every US team, by
   league and division. Use it to build outreach and to time content around events.
7. **Authority / influencer nodes.** In a niche this small, one respected US coach or pro
   endorsing "the read" moves the whole market. Seed a short list.
8. **Discord / team comms.** Younger players organize here; grassroots channel.

*(The prior "home-first / Europe" sequencing is removed — Europe is access-locked. Focus is
the US, with Canada / LatAm as clean secondary ground.)*

## 8. How the foundation drives execution

So this isn't theory on a shelf — every piece of content traces back to it:

- **Every post belongs to a pillar** ("Read the ___"). If it doesn't, it doesn't ship.
- **Every post = show real use + one concept + one image.** Demonstration, not assertion —
  the content thesis is "watch it work," let the market judge.
- **Show the output and the tool; do *not* teach the coaching craft.** Show *what the app
  does* (capture → consolidate → legible read, real screens). Don't publish a standalone
  how-to on reading opponents/tendencies — that's the methodology, and for three reasons
  (appetite + edge + Europe-can-watch) it stays in-house.
- **No hard waitlist yet (Jacek's call).** Soft CTA — follow / DM / "more reads coming."
  Audience traction (saves, shares, "is this an app?" DMs) *is* the interest signal. When
  people start asking how to get it, that's the trigger to open signups — the audience tells
  us when; we don't guess.
- **🔒 No European invites while Ranger.** Operational guardrail, repeated here so it never
  slips: access is US-centric / non-Europe, enforced via magic-link control.
- **The product reveals itself.** Weeks of teaching the five pillars using real surfaces;
  then "Read the game" as the payoff that shows the whole loop. By reveal time, the serious
  US teams already want in.
